Tracking renewables tripling

At the UN’s COP28 climate change conference in December 2023, governments agreed a goal to triple global renewable capacity by 2030.

According to independent analysis by the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) and the International Energy Agency (IEA), reaching at least 11,000 gigawatts (GW) of renewable capacity by 2030 could deliver up to two-thirds of the emissions reductions needed by 2050 to keep a 1.5°C pathway within reach.

Renewable additions have continued to rise since the COP28 agreement, with record deployment for both solar and wind in 2025 pointing to clear momentum. But renewables deployment still needs to increase year on year through 2030 to stay on track.

Global Energy Monitor’s facility-level tracking across the energy sector provides a detailed view of progress toward this critical goal.

According to GEM data, more than 5,500 utility-scale renewables projects are set to come online before 2030, totaling 1,240 GW — equal to about 20% of the capacity additions needed for tripling.

Another 20,000+ projects are in development without a confirmed start date, totaling 3,854 GW.

If all are completed in time, the world could meet the tripling target. But delays, cancellations, and competing demand for power mean that outcome is far from certain.

To stay on track, the renewable pipeline must keep expanding — and projects already underway must be delivered on time.

Small-scale renewable projects — mostly distributed solar — will also contribute to the total. While distributed solar will play a crucial role, it cannot fully compensate for shortfalls in utility-scale development.

Tripling renewables globally does not mean every country will triple its own capacity. Countries started from very different levels in the 2022 base year, so the scale of change required varies.

Countries with a large existing renewables base will see smaller relative increases. Brazil, for example, already has substantial utility-scale hydropower. Countries starting from a smaller base, meanwhile, could multiply capacity several times over by 2030 — not necessarily because of especially high ambition, but simply because they are starting from a lower level.

Still, independent assessments show that increases of tripling, or greater, are needed for a 1.5C-aligned pathway, especially in the largest economies. These countries would need to scale renewables by roughly 2.5 to 3.1 times by 2030.

So far, some of the richest economies are falling short. Even if all in-development renewable capacity in the G7 and EU were completed by 2030, total capacity would increase by only 1.8 times in the G7 and 2.2 times in the EU.

Fossil fuel phaseout

Equally critical to achieving a net-zero energy system is the rapid phase out of fossil fuels, with unabated coal plants being phased out by 2030 in advanced economies and by 2040 globally, while unabated natural gas declines by over 80% by 2040, according to the IEA.
